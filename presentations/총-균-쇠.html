Prologue

Yali's Question ü§î

The question that sparked the entire book, posed by a New Guinean politician named Yali.

"Why is it that you white people developed so much cargo...?"

"...and brought it to New Guinea, but we black people had little cargo of our own?"

This simple, yet profound question challenges common assumptions about human history. It's not about biological differences, but about the divergent paths societies took.

The Core Inquiry

Why did wealth and power become distributed as they are now, and not in some other way? Why did Eurasians conquer or displace Native Americans, Australians, and Africans, and not the reverse?

Part 1: From Eden to Cajamarca

Up to the Starting Line üèÉ

Examining human history from our evolutionary origins to the end of the last Ice Age (around 11,000 B.C.), setting the stage for different developmental trajectories.

The Great Leap Forward

Around 50,000 years ago, a burst of creativity and innovation appeared, marking the behavioral modernity of humans.

Innovations üí°

  • Standardized stone tools
  • First preserved jewelry (ostrich-shell beads)
  • Complex tools from bone (needles, awls)
  • Sophisticated weaponry (harpoons, spear-throwers)
  • Art, such as cave paintings and statues

Expansion üó∫Ô∏è

  • This leap enabled humans to expand across the globe.
  • Colonization of Australia and New Guinea (~40,000 BC), requiring watercraft.
  • Colonization of Siberia (~20,000 BC), requiring adaptation to extreme cold.
  • Finally, the Americas (~11,000 BC).

Part 1: Chapter 2

A Natural Experiment of History: Polynesia

The colonization of the Polynesian islands offers a perfect small-scale test for how environments shape societies.

Descendants of a single founding society diversified dramatically over a few thousand years, adapting to the vastly different environments of the islands they settled.

The Fates of the Moriori & Maori

A tragic collision between two societies descended from the same ancestors.

The Moriori (Chatham Islands) üèùÔ∏è

Settled a small, cold, isolated island group. Could not practice tropical agriculture. They reverted to a hunter-gatherer lifestyle, abandoned warfare, and limited their population. They developed simple technology and a peaceful, egalitarian society.

The Maori (New Zealand) üèîÔ∏è

Settled a large, diverse land suitable for their agriculture. Developed dense populations, sophisticated technology, and strong political organization. They were fiercely territorial and engaged in chronic warfare. In 1835, they invaded the Chatham Islands and annihilated the peaceful Moriori.

Part 1: Chapter 3

Collision at Cajamarca ‚öîÔ∏è

How did Pizarro's tiny force of 168 Spaniards defeat the Inca Emperor Atahualpa and his army of 80,000?

This event serves as a microcosm of European conquests, showcasing the immense power of proximate factors that gave them an advantage.

Why Pizarro Triumphed

üê¥ Military Technology: Spanish horses provided speed, surprise, and an intimidating platform. Steel swords, armor, and guns gave them a lethal advantage over the Incas' stone and bronze weapons.

ü¶† Epidemic Diseases: Eurasian germs, like smallpox, had already devastated the Inca Empire, killing the previous emperor and his heir, which triggered a debilitating civil war that Atahualpa had just won.

üö¢ Maritime Technology: Pizarro arrived in ships that were the product of centuries of European maritime development, allowing him to cross the Atlantic.

üèõÔ∏è Centralized Political Organization: Spain's state organization funded, staffed, and equipped the expedition. It provided a framework for conquest.

üìú Writing: Literacy allowed for the rapid and accurate transmission of information. Pizarro knew about Cort√©s's successful conquest of the Aztecs and used his tactics as a blueprint.

Part 2: The Rise of Food Production

The Power of Farming üåæ

This section explores the ultimate cause behind the inequalities of power: the transition from hunting and gathering to food production.

Farmer Power: An Indirect Chain of Causation

Food production didn't directly lead to guns and steel. Instead, it enabled a series of societal changes.

1

More Food, More People

Farming yields far more calories per acre than hunting-gathering, supporting much denser populations.

2

Sedentary Lifestyle

Farmers stay in one place. This allows for the accumulation of possessions and the development of more complex technology.

3

Specialists and Elites

Food surpluses can feed non-food-producing specialists: kings, bureaucrats, priests, artisans, and soldiers.

4

Development of Germs, Tools, and Writing

Domesticated animals were the source of epidemic diseases. Sedentary, dense societies fostered technological innovation and the need for writing systems to manage complex information.

History's Haves and Have-Nots

Why did food production arise independently in only a few parts of the world?

Independent Origins üå±

Food production began independently in at least five, possibly as many as nine, core areas:

  • Fertile Crescent (~8500 BC)
  • China (~7500 BC)
  • Mesoamerica (~3500 BC)
  • Andes/Amazonia (~3500 BC)
  • Eastern United States (~2500 BC)

Diffusion ‚û°Ô∏è

In most other places, agriculture was not independently invented but was imported from these core areas. Some societies, like those in Australia, never adopted food production at all before the modern era.

To Farm or Not to Farm?

The shift to farming was not a conscious choice for a "better life." It was a gradual, often unintentional process.

Early farming was often harder and less healthy than hunting-gathering. The boundary between the two lifestyles was blurry. Several factors tipped the balance:

  • Decline of wild foods: Over-hunting and climate change made wild resources less reliable.
  • Availability of domesticable plants: Some wild plants were easier to grow and harvest, making cultivation more attractive.
  • Technological advances: New technologies for collecting, processing, and storing food made farming more efficient.
  • Rising population density: A slow rise in population pushed people toward producing more food from a given area of land.

How to Make an Almond üå∞

The story of plant domestication is one of unconscious selection.

Many of our most important crops started as unpromising, even poisonous, wild plants. Early farmers inadvertently selected for desirable traits without understanding genetics.

The Case of the Almond

Wild almonds are bitter and deadly, containing cyanide. A rare genetic mutation produces sweet, non-poisonous almonds. Early gatherers would have tasted and discarded the bitter nuts but eaten and spread the seeds of the few sweet ones they found, eventually leading to the domesticated almond we know today.

The Anna Karenina Principle üêæ

"Happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way."

Jared Diamond applies this principle to animal domestication. A candidate for domestication must possess a suite of essential traits. A failure in just one trait can make domestication impossible.

This explains why out of 148 large, terrestrial herbivorous mammal species, only 14 were ever domesticated.

The Domestication Checklist ‚úÖ

Why have so few large mammals been domesticated? They must pass a tough six-point test.

  • 1. Diet: Must be efficient to feed (herbivores/omnivores).
  • 2. Growth Rate: Must grow quickly enough to be economically viable.
  • 3. Captive Breeding: Must be willing to breed in captivity.
  • 4. Nasty Disposition: Must not be overly dangerous or aggressive. (Why we don't ride zebras or keep grizzly bears).
  • 5. Tendency to Panic: Must not have a strong flight instinct when enclosed.
  • 6. Social Structure: Must live in herds with a clear dominance hierarchy that humans can co-opt.

Spacious Skies and Tilted Axes ‚ÜîÔ∏è‚ÜïÔ∏è

The orientation of continents played a massive role in the spread of food production.

Eurasia's East-West Axis

Locations at the same latitude share similar day lengths, seasons, climates, and diseases. This allowed crops, livestock, and technologies from the Fertile Crescent to spread rapidly across the entire continent, from Europe to China.

Americas' & Africa's North-South Axis

Travel north or south brings drastic changes in climate. A crop adapted to Mexico would fail in Canada. This created major ecological barriers, drastically slowing the diffusion of agriculture and technology.

Part 3: From Food to Steel

Lethal Gifts: Guns, Germs, & Steel

Connecting the ultimate cause (food production) to the proximate causes that enabled conquest.

The Lethal Gift of Livestock ü¶†

The greatest killers of humankind‚Äîsmallpox, influenza, measles, tuberculosis‚Äîevolved from diseases of domesticated herd animals.

Eurasians lived in close proximity to their animals for millennia. This constant exposure allowed animal microbes to jump to humans and evolve into epidemic diseases. Over time, Eurasian populations developed significant immunities.

A One-Sided Exchange

When Europeans arrived in the Americas, they brought these germs with them. Native American populations had no domesticated herd animals (besides the llama), no exposure, and no immunity. The resulting epidemics killed up to 95% of the pre-Columbian population, proving more devastating than any army.

Blueprints and Borrowed Letters üìú

Writing was another major advantage, but it arose independently only a few times in history.

Independent Invention

  • Sumerians (Mesopotamia, ~3000 BC)
  • Mesoamericans (Mexico, ~600 BC)
  • Egyptian and Chinese writing may also be independent inventions.

Idea Diffusion

Most societies acquired writing by borrowing from neighbors (blueprint copying) or by being inspired by the idea of writing to create their own system (idea diffusion), like the Cherokee script.

The east-west axis of Eurasia facilitated the rapid spread of writing systems, unlike in the Americas.

Necessity's Mother? üí°

The common saying is often reversed: "Invention is the mother of necessity."

Many inventions were developed by curious tinkerers without a clear use in mind. Only later was a "need" found for them. Think of the first automobile or Edison's phonograph.

Factors Driving Technology

Technology develops cumulatively and is driven by societal factors:

  • Food production and a sedentary life are prerequisites for complex technology.
  • Large, dense, competitive societies are the most innovative.
  • Geographic connectivity allows societies to adopt innovations from others. Isolation stifles it. Eurasia's large size and east-west axis gave it a huge advantage.

From Egalitarianism to Kleptocracy üëë

How did large, centralized, unequal societies (kleptocracies) evolve from small, kin-based groups?

As populations grew, societies evolved through four stages: Band -> Tribe -> Chiefdom -> State.

Larger populations require more complex systems for conflict resolution, decision-making, and economy. Elites arise to manage these systems, but how do they persuade the commoners to give up their resources?

  • Disarm the public and arm the elite.
  • Redistribute tribute in popular ways.
  • Use a monopoly on force to maintain public order.
  • Construct an ideology or religion that justifies the kleptocracy.

Part 4: Around the World

Testing the Theory: Continental Case Studies

Applying the framework of ultimate and proximate causes to understand the specific histories of Australia, New Guinea, China, the Americas, and Africa.

Australia and New Guinea: A Tale of Two Halves

Both started from the same human stock on the same continent, but their fates diverged dramatically after sea levels rose.

Australia ü¶ò

The driest, flattest, and least fertile continent. Lacked native plants and animals suitable for domestication (megafauna went extinct). Remained a continent of hunter-gatherers with simple technology and social organization.

New Guinea üê¶

Wet, rugged, and mountainous. Independently developed agriculture (taro, bananas, sugarcane). Supported dense farming populations. But, like Australia, it lacked domesticable large mammals and cereal crops, limiting protein, land transport, and the development of larger political units.

How China Became Chinese üá®üá≥

China provides a stark contrast to Europe: early and persistent political and cultural unity.

China's geography facilitated this unity. It's not transected by major mountain ranges like the Alps or Pyrenees. Its two major river systems (Yellow and Yangtze) are connected by easily navigable lowlands and were eventually linked by canals.

A Double-Edged Sword

This connectivity allowed for rapid diffusion of culture and technology, and easy political unification. However, it also meant that a single bad decision by one ruler could halt innovation across the entire civilization, as happened when the Ming Dynasty decided to scrap its ocean-going treasure fleets in the 15th century.

Speedboat to Polynesia: The Austronesian Expansion

One of the most extensive human migrations in history, spreading from South China/Taiwan across the vast Pacific and Indian Oceans.

This expansion was driven by a superior package of technology and food production:

  • Advanced maritime technology: Outrigger canoes capable of long-distance voyages.
  • A robust agricultural package: Root crops like taro and yam, tree crops like breadfruit and banana, and domesticated animals (pigs, chickens, dogs).
  • This package allowed them to outcompete or replace the hunter-gatherer populations of the Philippines and Indonesia, and to settle the previously uninhabited islands of the Pacific.

Hemispheres Colliding, Revisited üí•

Why did the collision between Eurasia and the Americas play out so one-sidedly?

Eurasia's Head Start

  • Longer history of human settlement.
  • Vastly superior set of domesticable plants and animals.
  • East-west axis facilitating rapid spread.
  • Result: Denser populations, earlier states, more advanced technology, and deadlier germs.

The Americas' Disadvantages

  • Shorter history of human settlement.
  • Paucity of domesticable large mammals after the Ice Age extinction.
  • North-south axis and geographic barriers slowing diffusion.
  • Result: Vulnerability to Eurasian conquest.

How Africa Became Black üåç

Africa's immense human diversity is the result of a long and complex history shaped by its unique geography.

Africa is home to 5 of the world's 6 major human groups. Its history is dominated by the Bantu Expansion, a massive migration of farmers originating from West Africa around 3000 BC.

The Bantu Advantage

Armed with iron tools and a robust agricultural package (yams, sorghum), Bantu-speaking farmers expanded over millennia, displacing or absorbing the indigenous Khoisan and Pygmy hunter-gatherers across most of sub-equatorial Africa. This expansion was slowed only by ecological barriers, like the different climate zone in South Africa.

Conclusion: History as a Science

The broad patterns of history are not a story of heroic individuals or cultural superiority. They are the result of environmental differences amplified over time.

The fates of human societies were decisively shaped by differences in geography and biogeography‚Äîthe ultimate factors that determined the availability of domesticable species and the ease of diffusion.
