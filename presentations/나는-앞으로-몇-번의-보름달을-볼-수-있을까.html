How Many More Full Moons Will I See?

The Final Story of Ryuichi Sakamoto

Who is Ryuichi Sakamoto? üéπ

A world-renowned musician, composer, and activist born in Tokyo in 1952.

Pioneer: He made his solo debut in 1978 with Thousand Knives and formed the pioneering electronic music trio Yellow Magic Orchestra (YMO) in the same year.

Award-Winner: He won numerous awards for his film scores, including a BAFTA for Merry Christmas, Mr. Lawrence and an Academy Award, Golden Globe, and Grammy for The Last Emperor.

Activist: He was a passionate advocate for environmental and peace issues, founding the forest conservation organization 'more trees' and supporting young musicians from disaster-stricken areas through the 'Tohoku Youth Orchestra'.

He passed away on March 28, 2023, leaving behind a profound legacy.

Part 1: The Final Chapter

Living with Cancer

The book begins with a profound question that shaped Sakamoto's final years, turning an abstract line from a film into a daily, personal meditation.

"How many more full moons will I see?"

This question, which became the title of his memoir, originated from a line spoken by author Paul Bowles in the film The Sheltering Sky.

For a long time, the line remained a powerful but distant quote. However, after his first cancer diagnosis in 2014, it became a deeply personal reflection on his own mortality.

The Original Quote

"Because we don‚Äôt know when we will die, we get to think of life as an inexhaustible well... How many more times will you watch the full moon rise? Perhaps twenty. And yet it all seems limitless."

From Quote to Composition üé∂

This meditation on mortality directly inspired the track "fullmoon" on his 2017 album, async.

A Global Chorus

Sakamoto sampled the quote and had it translated and read by native speakers in various languages, creating a global chorus contemplating the same question.

Bertolucci's Final Performance

For the final Italian narration, he casually asked director Bernardo Bertolucci. Bertolucci agreed immediately, recording the lines that would become one of his last vocal performances before his death a year later.

A Difficult Journey

Sakamoto provides a frank and detailed account of his battle with cancer.

  • 2014: First diagnosed with oropharyngeal (throat) cancer, which was successfully treated.
  • June 2020: Diagnosed with rectal cancer during a check-up in New York.
  • December 2020: Discovered in Japan that the cancer had metastasized to his liver and lymph nodes‚Äîa fact his New York hospital had failed to communicate. This led to a profound loss of trust.
  • January 2021: Underwent a massive, 20-hour surgery in Japan to remove multiple tumors.

Confronting the Prognosis

After discovering the metastasis, he was given a blunt prognosis by a Japanese doctor.

"You have about 6 months left."

He was shocked and angered by the doctor's tone, which left no room for hope. It was a purely statistical, objective assessment that felt devoid of humanity.

Incredibly, the very next day, Sakamoto performed a pre-scheduled online solo piano concert. He felt he played poorly, his mind in turmoil, but those who knew him well praised the performance, finding a unique depth in it.

The Mind's Labyrinth üß†

After the 20-hour surgery, he experienced several bouts of intense delirium, a common side effect of general anesthesia.

  • He woke up convinced he was in a regional hospital in Korea, trying to communicate with nurses in broken Korean.
  • He messaged his assistant, "I'm going to be late for the meeting," while unable to move his body.
  • He was haunted by a continuous loop of an old Japanese TV commercial jingle he didn't even like.
  • Though frightening, he also found the experience fascinating, a glimpse into the surreal, automatic, and vast storage of the subconscious brain.

Saved by Love ‚ù§Ô∏è

Recovery was a difficult process, marked by physical pain, complications, and emotional despair.

Fighting Back

Nurses urged him to get out of bed and walk to prevent muscle atrophy. Despite the pain and weakness, he forced himself to sit on the sofa, read, and listen to music.

"Romi-Julie"

Due to COVID-19 restrictions, his partner couldn't visit. Instead, every evening they would wave their smartphone flashlights to each other across the street‚Äîa ritual they nicknamed 'Romi-Julie' (Romeo and Juliet).

Questioning Time

Sakamoto's illness intensified his lifelong philosophical inquiry into the nature of time.

He argues that music is often called a "time-based art," progressing along a linear path. However, his experiences led him to question this premise.

Time as an Illusion

After years of reading philosophy and physics, he concluded that the concept of 'absolute time'‚Äîa constant, forward-moving force independent of the observer‚Äîis an illusion created by the human brain.

The Impulse to Destroy

A surprising experience after the premiere of his theatrical work TIME at the Holland Festival.

After years of work, he watched the "final" performance and suddenly felt a powerful urge to destroy it. He has always disliked "formal" or fixed things, and the idea of a work being "complete" and static felt like a creative death. This impulse was a new and startling experience for him, a testament to his desire for art to remain a living, changing entity.

The Shadow of a Masterpiece

He discusses his complicated relationship with his most famous work.

A 30-Second Flash

The iconic melody came to him in a flash of inspiration, taking only about 30 seconds to form in his mind at the piano.

Acceptance

For over 10 years, he refused to play the piece in concert, tired of its overwhelming popularity. He finally came to terms with its importance to his audience after seeing Carole King perform "You've Got A Friend" and realizing the joy it brought people.

Life's True Form

Sakamoto's thoughts on accepting the natural course of life and death.

He reflects that in a pre-modern era, he would have died at 62 with his first cancer diagnosis, and that would have been considered a long life. The relentless modern pursuit of prolonging life at any cost felt questionable to him.

A Natural End

He expresses admiration for the philosophy of "Today is a good day to die," a mindset that embraces death as a natural part of life, not a failure to be fought off with painful treatments. While he still underwent treatment, he fundamentally believed in living and dying naturally.

Part 2: Music and Memory

Requiem for a Mother

This section explores the influences and experiences that shaped his music after 2009, including travel, loss, and artistic breakthroughs.

Music Sets You Free üïäÔ∏è

The title of his 2009 autobiography, "Ongaku wa Jiyuu ni Suru" (Music Makes You Free), was a deliberate commentary.

It's a satirical twist on the infamous Nazi slogan "Arbeit macht frei" (Work sets you free). For Sakamoto, music was the true source of liberation.

This idea became even more potent for him in the face of political turmoil like 9/11 and his own personal "prison" of cancer. Even when the body is not free, the act of creating and listening to music offers moments of escape from pain and suffering.

Journey to the Arctic

A 2008 trip to Greenland as part of the "Cape Farewell" art-and-science project became a pivotal experience.

The Sound of Nature

Seeing the aurora borealis and the massive icebergs, some formed 20,000 years ago, gave him a profound sense of nature's sublime power and humanity's insignificance.

Field Recordings

The journey directly inspired his album Out of Noise. He made numerous field recordings, capturing the sounds of melting ice and even ringing a bell inside an ice cave.

Entering "The Zone"

During a grueling European tour in 2009, he had a breakthrough experience at Cadogan Hall in London.

He describes it as something athletes call "being in the zone." For two hours, he played in a state of "no-mind" (ÁÑ°Êàë), where all conscious thought disappeared and his fingers moved on their own. He made no mistakes.

A New Level

His partner, who had watched countless performances, told him that his playing fundamentally changed from that night onward. It was a rare and profound moment of transcendence where it felt as if "the god of music descended from heaven."

A Sunflower-like Mother üåª

He fondly remembers his mother, Keiko, who passed away in 2010.

Contrast and Balance

He contrasts his quiet, scholarly father with his bright, fashionable, and sociable mother. He felt this duality in his parents sometimes led to a split in his own personality.

First Movie Memory

A passionate fan of Italian cinema, she took him to see his very first film as a child: Federico Fellini's La Strada. He couldn't remember the plot, but the main theme song remained etched in his memory.

The Cycle of Seasons

Shortly after his mother's death, he composed a Concerto for Koto and Orchestra.

The piece was inspired by the idea that seasons truly begin in winter (a time for seeds to grow) and end in autumn. This cycle reminded him of the cycle of human life.

A Tacit Requiem

Though he never stated it publicly, he reveals that the concerto was a requiem for his mother. The piece begins in the quiet of winter and flows towards its end in autumn, mirroring his thoughts of her during its creation.

Part 3: Art and Activism

You Can't Fight Nature

The 3/11 Great East Japan Earthquake and Fukushima nuclear disaster marked a pivotal moment, deepening his commitment to activism and reshaping his artistic philosophy.

March 11, 2011 üåä

He was in a Tokyo recording studio when the massive earthquake struck.

His first instinct, he amusingly notes, was to save the expensive microphone before hiding under the desk. The experience of the long, unfamiliar shaking was terrifying.

In the following days, the horror unfolded with news of the tsunami and the subsequent hydrogen explosions at the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant. He watched in horror as the worst-case scenario he had been warning against for years became a reality.

The "Tsunami Piano"

In the disaster's aftermath, he discovered a piano that had been submerged in the tsunami.

The instrument was completely broken, but he was fascinated by the sound it made. He felt it was the sound of a man-made object being forcibly returned to its natural state.

Nature's Reclaiming

"The tsunami was the result of the force of nature, so the piano was a victim of it. You could even say that the piano was tuned by nature."

He rescued the piano, and its unique sounds were featured heavily on his album async and in the installation piece IS YOUR TIME.

Connection with Korea üá∞üá∑

He details his long and warm relationship with Korea.

Mutual Respect

During his first major concert in 2000, he was impressed by the humility and ambition of Korean music professionals. He felt a sense of camaraderie as a fellow Asian artist.

A Fan of Hallyu

He became a huge fan of the Korean Wave, from dramas like Winter Sonata and Mr. Sunshine to films like Parasite and artists like BTS, watching them frequently on Netflix.

'more trees' and LIFE311

His environmental activism took on new urgency after the 3/11 disaster.

Through his 'more trees' foundation, he launched the 'LIFE311' project to build temporary wooden houses for tsunami victims in Rikuzentakata, using timber from the nearby forestry town of Sumita.

He was outraged when bureaucracy initially blocked funding. 'more trees' decided to raise the 300 million yen itself, successfully gathering around 240 million yen and inspiring local officials to push back against the government.

"It's Just Electricity"

He addresses the controversy over a speech he gave at an anti-nuclear rally.

The media focused on his phrase "Taka ga denki" (It's just electricity), leading to immense criticism. He clarifies his point was never to belittle electricity's value, but to ask a simple question:

The Core Question

"Between human life and electricity, which is more important?" The answer, he states, should obviously be 'life'. His argument was that we should not risk lives for the most dangerous form of power generation when safer, renewable alternatives exist.

Part 4: Inspiration and Creation

Travel and Creation

Exploring the world, meeting other artists, and finding inspiration in unexpected places were central to Sakamoto's creative process.

Learning from Iceland üáÆüá∏

A 2013 trip to Iceland for the Sonar festival left a deep impression.

A Model for the Future

He was fascinated by how Iceland recovered from the 2008 financial crisis by rejecting endless economic growth and holding its bankers accountable. He saw it as a model of sustainability.

100% Renewable Energy

He was inspired by Iceland's use of 70% hydropower and 30% geothermal power. He was surprised to find the geothermal turbines were made by Mitsubishi, lamenting that Japan, a volcanic nation, wasn't doing the same.

"I Hate Sightseeing!" üò©

Sakamoto makes a clear distinction between traveling for work and being a tourist.

He recounts a story from Portugal where a well-meaning record label rep took him on an all-day tour. After hours of being stuck in traffic, his patience snapped.

An Explosive Moment

He jumped out of the car, yelled "I hate sightseeing!" at the stunned rep, and walked all the way back to his hotel. He later felt terrible and apologized, but the story illustrates his intense focus on work over leisure.

Encounters with Giants

He recalls his memorable meetings with two of his idols, Nam June Paik and John Cage.

  • Nam June Paik (1984): He was deeply moved when Paik, whom he revered, greeted him warmly like an old friend in Tokyo. He later visited Paik's spartan, roof-leaking studio in New York.
  • John Cage: With Paik, he visited the home of composer John Cage. Cage, a mushroom expert, shared a story about losing all his possessions three times while traveling. Each time, he saw it not as a loss, but as a welcome opportunity to reset his life, a Zen-like attitude that impressed Sakamoto.

The World of Film Festivals üé¨

Serving as a jury member at festivals like Venice and Berlin was a gateway to a new world.

It was Bernardo Bertolucci who first invited him to be a juror. This world provided an incredible opportunity to meet other filmmakers, discover unknown films from around the globe, and engage in a concentrated, high-level dialogue about cinema that he found was missing in the more siloed music industry. He formed lasting friendships with directors like Taiwan's Tsai Ming-liang.

Part 5: Confronting Limits

The First Frustration

This section details Sakamoto's first encounters with the physical limitations of aging and the immense challenge of his return to work after cancer treatment.

The First Sign: Presbyopia

At age 42, he experienced the first undeniable sign of aging.

In a recording studio, he suddenly found he couldn't focus on the sheet music in his hands. The notes were a blur. For someone who had always had 1.5 (20/13) vision, the experience was a profound shock.

Moving the score back and forth, he found the focal point had shifted further away. He had to get reading glasses, and he recalls that seeing the world through them for the first time felt like entering a different dimension, making him wonder what else he had been missing.

A Fateful Prophecy

A few years later, a casual moment at a party with musician Taeko Onuki took a serious turn.

She playfully asked to read his palm, but her expression suddenly hardened. In a dead serious tone, she told him:

"You won't live much longer if you continue like this."

The warning was so sincere that it spurred him to explore alternative health practices for the first time, including Noguchi Seitai (a form of Japanese bodywork) and a macrobiotic diet. It was a crucial turning point in his approach to his own health.

The Revenant: A Painful Return

His first major project after his 2014 cancer treatment was scoring Alejandro I√±√°rritu's The Revenant.

The offer came when he was still recovering. A music supervisor on the film, who had also survived cancer, told him, "Getting back to work is the best thing for you."

His partner was even more blunt: "How many people in the world get a direct request from I√±√°rritu? It's fine if the cancer comes back and you die, just do it." It was, he says, a cruel but effective push.

A Taste of Failure

Working on The Revenant was the first time in his life he felt he had truly failed.

He was battling not only physical exhaustion but also "chemo brain," a cognitive fog that made concentration difficult. For the first time, he felt his physical and mental abilities couldn't match his creative ambitions.

"I couldn't give 100%."

Several of his pieces were rejected in favor of the temp music, and he didn't have the energy to fight for them or create better alternatives. The experience was deeply humbling and left him with a lasting sense of regret.

One Last Stand: "Trust me!"

There was one scene where he refused to back down.

For a pivotal, dreamlike scene where the protagonist reunites with his deceased son, I√±√°rritu wanted to use the temporary guide music. Sakamoto fought hard for his own composition, which he felt was superior.

In a final, desperate plea, he simply told the demanding director, "Trust me!" and pushed through with the recording.

His piece was chosen. The victory was so sweet that he later made "Trust me!" t-shirts for the entire music team.

Part 6: A New Direction

Towards a Bigger Mountain

After the struggles of The Revenant, Sakamoto found a new creative path, culminating in his 2017 masterpiece, async.

The Genesis of async

His first original album in 8 years, it was a radical departure from his previous work.

Rejecting the Brain's Habit

Inspired by the "Mono-ha" art movement, which focused on materials in their unaltered state, he wanted to create music that defied the brain's habit of finding patterns and meaning.

Imaginary Soundtrack

The central concept became creating a "soundtrack for an imaginary Andrei Tarkovsky film." Tarkovsky himself believed films didn't need added music, as the recorded soundscape was already musical. Sakamoto embraced this idea.

The Sound of async

He collected sounds from various sources, focusing on their raw, physical properties.

  • He recorded the sounds of stones he found on the streets of New York.
  • He recorded the sound of rain on his garden's glass roof during a downpour.
  • He captured the unique resonance of sound sculptures by Harry Bertoia.
  • Most famously, he used the sounds of the "Tsunami Piano."

The result was a non-linear, asynchronous listening experience designed for a 3D space, challenging the very idea of a conventional album.

A New Path Found

Completing async was a profound personal and artistic victory.

For most of his career, he felt he lacked a signature "Sakamoto sound," unlike artists such as Brian Eno. He had always just pursued whatever interested him at the moment.

"I don't want to lose what I've gained here."

With async, he felt he had finally discovered a new mountain to climb. The album was so personal he joked he didn't want anyone else to hear it. From this point on, he decided to nurture and expand upon the world he had created with this album.

Part 7: Passing the Torch

Meeting New Talents

In his later years, Sakamoto found great joy and inspiration in connecting with a new generation of artists who had been influenced by his work.

A New Generation of Admirers

He was surprised and delighted to meet younger artists who considered him a "sensei."

  • Flying Lotus & Thundercat: He was shocked that leading figures of the 21st-century Black music scene were deeply influenced by him. He collaborated with Flying Lotus and fondly recalls Thundercat's "otaku" passion for Dragon Ball.
  • Oneohtrix Point Never (OPN): He bonded with Daniel Lopatin over a shared love for Tarkovsky and analog synthesizers.
  • Se So Neon (ÏÉàÏÜåÎÖÑ): He was instantly captivated by the Korean indie band's leader, Hwang So-yoon, and her guitar playing, striking up a friendship despite their 40-year age gap.

Meeting SUGA of BTS

In September 2022, he met with Suga in Tokyo.

He was impressed by the global superstar's humility and sincere dedication to music. Suga shared that his musical journey began at age 12 after watching a re-release of The Last Emperor and becoming fascinated by the score.

This unexpected connection, born from a work he created decades ago, led to a new collaboration: Sakamoto provided a piano arrangement for "Snooze," a track on Suga's solo album.

Part 8: The Final Works

Leaving for the Future

Knowing his time was limited, Sakamoto focused on creating a definitive record of his work for posterity.

Playing the Piano 2022

Dissatisfied with his 2020 online concert performed in the depths of his illness, he decided to record one last, perfect piano solo.

He booked NHK's 509 Studio in Tokyo, which he considered to have the best acoustics in Japan. The project was filmed with meticulous care, with lighting and camera positions changing for each song.

The performance was a final chance to present his music with the strength he had left. He acknowledged that he no longer had the stamina for a live concert, but found peace in having "left behind a performance I could be satisfied with before I die."

The Final Album: 12

Released on his 71st birthday, January 17, 2023, 12 is an intimate "sound diary."

Musical Sketches

While recovering from his 2021 surgery, he would occasionally play his synthesizer and piano, simply wanting to be enveloped in sound. He recorded these sketches without any grand concept, titling them only with the date they were recorded.

Raw and Unplanned

Unlike his other highly conceptual albums, 12 is raw, unplanned, and immediate. He notes, "Right now, for me, this kind of raw, unadorned music feels more satisfying." It is the sound of an artist in his final days, communing directly with sound itself.

From the Diary

The epilogue shares excerpts from his final diary entries, revealing his state of mind.

  • Oct 11, 2022: "Living is a pain."
  • Nov 21, 2021: "Smash the walls!"
  • Dec 27, 2021: "When everyone's ego disappears, you can give a good performance."
  • Apr 18, 2022: "Now that it has come to this, I am prepared to accept any fate."
  • Mar 24, 2023: "No energy."

The Final Days

The epilogue, written by editor Masafumi Suzuki, details Sakamoto's last months with profound respect.

  • He continued working, creating a final piece of music for his friends at the DUMB TYPE art collective.
  • He created a 'Funeral Playlist' of songs to be played at his own service.
  • He watched over the Tohoku Youth Orchestra's performance remotely from his hospital bed, conducting with his arm in the air.
  • On March 28, 2023, at 4:32 AM, he passed away at the age of 71.
